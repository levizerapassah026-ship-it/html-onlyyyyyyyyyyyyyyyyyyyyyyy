✦ The Art of Living Aesthetically

Living aesthetically isn't just about how things look — it's about how they make you feel. According to Chantelle Grady, to live an aesthetic life means going through life often focused on beauty: creating it in our surroundings and noticing it in our everyday moments.

Research has even shown that surrounding ourselves with visually pleasing environments can reduce stress levels and increase feelings of happiness and calmness. Beauty doesn't have to be expensive or complicated. A cozy corner with soft lighting, a few plants, and your favorite mug — that's already a little masterpiece.

As Cozychris.com puts it, an aesthetic lifestyle is not about superficial living where looks reign supreme — it's about a beauty so profound on the inside that it shines on the outside. When we pay attention to the small details around us, everyday life starts to feel more intentional and more our own.

Source: chantellegrady.com  |  cozychris.com

✦ Music as My Escape

Music has always been a part of my life. No matter what I feel, there is always a song that understands me.

According to the American Psychiatric Association, music engagement plays a significant role in mood regulation — and research has shown it can have a beneficial effect on stress-related outcomes. Music can even trigger the release of dopamine, a feel-good neurotransmitter that influences our focus, memory, and overall mood.

What makes music so personal is that it connects to our emotions, memories, and experiences. As Tallahassee Memorial HealthCare explains, a song will not give everybody the same feeling — and that's the beauty of it. Music is deeply individual. Sometimes I discover a random song late at night, and somehow it becomes the soundtrack of that whole season of my life.

Source: psychiatry.org  |  tmh.org

✦ Finding Peace in Everyday Life

In the middle of busy schedules and endless to-do lists, it's easy to forget that rest is not a luxury — it's a necessity.

According to University Hospitals, rest and sleep are among the most important factors for maintaining both mental and physical health, yet most people don't prioritize them enough. Rest doesn't always mean sleep — it can mean doing less, moving gently, or simply taking five quiet minutes to breathe and just be.

As Rogers Behavioral Health notes, when we're truly resting, we give our minds and bodies the opportunity to pause and recharge. These quiet moments — a warm drink, a slow walk, journaling before bed — remind us that life is not just something to get through. It's something to actually live, softly and with intention.

Source: uhhospitals.org  |  rogersbh.org
